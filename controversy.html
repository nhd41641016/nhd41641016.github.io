                  Controversy

                  The mass media, including interactive social-networking tools, make you passive, can sap your initiative, leave you content to watch the spectacle of life from your couch or smartphone.

                  Apparently even during a revolution.

                  —Noam Cohen, New York Times

                  There are people who are against the wide-held belief of social media being the main catalyst for protest. In his paper entitled "Media Disruption Exacerbates Revolutionary Unrest," Dr. Navid Hassanpur argues that "Full connectivity in a social network can hinder collective actions." In fact, social media can also spread a message of caution, delay, or confusion, thus hindering the revolutionary process. One example is when Mubarak's regime shutdown the Internet and cell phone networks, there was proliferation of protests throughout Cairo.

                  Malcolm Gladwell on controversy about the role of social media in revolutions


                  "But any attempt to credit a massive political shift to a single factor - technological, economic, or otherwise - is simply untrue. Tunisians took to the streets due to decades of frustration, not in reaction to a WikiLeaks cable, a denial-of-service attack, or a Facebook update."

                  —Ethan Zuckerman
